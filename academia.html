Book chapters

Fire in the Anthropocene
Patrícia S. Silva, Renata Libonati, José A. Marengo, Mabel C. Costa, Lincoln M. Alves, Isabel B. Schmidt

Fire has been a natural disturbance for millions of years, influencing and shaping most terrestrial ecosystems on Earth. The dawn of the Anthropocene, an epoch in which humans represent a driving force within the Earth system, raises new questions and challenges for fire science. Humankind has been disrupting natural fire regimes in fire-prone regions and inducing fires in fire-sensitive ecosystems through increased ignition sources, fire suppression, and anthropogenic climate change. In South America, these dynamics are fundamental to understand current fire regimes, as anthropogenic burning now dominates vast regions across the continent. Associated with rising temperatures, changing rainfall patterns, and the increased frequency of extreme events, the heightened risk of wildfires poses significant challenges for various sectors and the overall well-being of the region. Events such as the Pantanal 2020 fires, in Brazil, or the 2024 Chilean fires, should not be evaluated through a single meteorological or anthropogenic perspective, and have been shown to be an interplay of both factors. In this chapter, we will delve on the fire regimes of the Anthropocene, and how humans directly and indirectly influence fire activity in South America.
Climate change and fire: the case of Cerrado, the Brazilian savanna
Patrícia S. Silva, Renata Libonati, Isabel B. Schmidt, Joana Nogueira, Carlos C. DaCamara

Stretching across central Brazil, Cerrado, harbours the most floristically diverse savannas in the world. Over the last decades, this biodiversity hotspot has undergone severe changes in land use and currently, less than 20% of its native vegetation cover remains undisturbed. One such disturbance is fire. As a fire-dependent ecosystem, Cerrado's plant and animal species have developed adaptations to fire, and its occurrence is paramount to the biome's ecological functioning. Cerrado presents a variety of fire dynamics over its 2 million km and thus its drivers and constraints are also diverse and highly dependent on regional context. However, changes in historical fire patterns and the increasing occurrence of wildfires severely damage the biome and risk ecosystem services. Future changes in climate will further promote favourable meteorological conditions for severe and out-of-season wildfires. In this chapter, we discuss these topics with a comprehensive literature review and contribute to understanding fire in Cerrado with novel results regarding seasonal occurrence, trends, and drivers.
